Four-Years Of Studies Evaluating Biological Fungicides For Powdery Mildew Management By Mixing And Phenologically Alternating With Sulfur

Brent Warneke1, Lloyd Nackley2, Jay Pscheidt3*

1Senior Faculty Research Assistant I & PhD student, Department of Botany and Plant Pathology, 2701 SW Campus Way, Corvallis, OR 97331, 2Associate Professor, Nursery Production, North Willamette Research and Extension Center, 40816 NE Miley Rd., Aurora, OR 97002, 3Extension Plant Pathology Specialist and Professor, Department of Botany and Plant Pathology, 2701 SW Campus Way, Corvallis, OR 97331

*Corresponding Author: jay.pscheidt@oregonstate.edu, 541-737-5539

Biological fungicides, those with living organisms, are organic compliant but expensive, and with variable efficacy. Alone, these products have been unable to adequately manage grape diseases such as powdery mildew. To evaluate biological fungicides, two application regimes for control of Grape Powdery Mildew (GPM) were tested from 2021-2024, tank mixes and phenological alternations with micronized sulfur. Tank mixes of biological fungicides with below-label-rate sulfur (2.5lb/A in 2021, 2lb/A in 2022-2024) were tested to determine if adding sulfur to biofungicides could improve GPM management over sulfur alone. The bacterial based products Actinovate, Serenade ASO, Theia, Lifegard, Aviv, Double Nickel, and Serifel were evaluated with sulfur. In 2021 Serenade ASO tank mixes resulted in 16.7%-19.7% lower cluster severity than sulfur alone. The Theia tank mix resulted in significantly lower cluster severity by 30.6% and 38% when compared with sulfur alone in 2022 and 2023, respectively, but only resulted in lower leaf incidence in 2023. In 2024, GPM severity on clusters was significantly lower for both Double Nickel and Serifel tank mixes compared to sulfur alone. For phenological applications, sulfur was applied only during the bloom period, while biological fungicides were applied before and after bloom. In 2021 Aviv or Serenade ASO treatments resulted in average cluster severities between 13.8% and 18.2%. Lifegard resulted in significantly lower cluster severity than the sulfur control in 2023. In 2022 and 2023, Theia resulted in a lower AUDPC value than the sulfur only treatment, while in 2023 all Theia treatments resulted in cluster severity values that were significantly lower than the sulfur only treatment. Serenade ASO, Theia, Double Nickel, and Serifel augmented GPM control over sulfur alone and could offer grape growers alternatives to sulfur or synthetic fungicides for GPM control.

Pruning Wound Protection Against Grapevine Trunk Disease Is Essential For The Pacific Northwest Vineyards.

Achala N. KC1,3, *, Anupa Gaire1,3, and Patricia A. Skinkis2, 4

1 Department of Botany and Plant Pathology, Oregon State University, 2082 Cordley Hall, Corvallis OR 97331

2 Department of Horticulture, Oregon State University, 4017 Agriculture and Life Sciences Building, Corvallis OR 97331

3 Southern Oregon Research and Extension Center, 569 Hanley Rd., Central Point, OR 97502

4Oregon Wine Research Institute, Oregon State University, 3051 SW Campus Way, Corvallis, OR 97331

*Corresponding author: achala.kc@oregonstate.edu; 541-930-8410

Grapevine trunk diseases (GTDs) are caused by many fungal pathogens that enter the trunk through pruning wounds, colonize and block the xylem vessels. This leads to reduced translocation of water and nutrients to the trunk, eventually leading to stunted shoot growth and vine decline. We identified Botryosphaeria dieback as the most common GTD complex present in western Oregon vineyards followed by Esca diseases based on two years of field survey data. Of the 29 surveyed vineyards in the Willamette and Rogue Valley, we detected Botryosphaeria and Esca pathogens in 72% and 21% of the vineyards, respectively. These results were based on culture and PCR methods. To advance our understanding of the fungal microbiome, the samples were assayed with DNA metabarcoding. We detected 32 different fungal genera, of which 19 were GTD-related pathogens. Two uncommon GTD genera Truncatella and Seimatosporium were detected from 100% of the samples. Phaeoacremonium and Phaeomoniella, two common Esca pathogens, were detected in nearly 70% of the samples and Botryosphaeria pathogens from nearly 60% of the samples. Under northern and southern Oregon conditions, Botryosphaeriaceae spores were detected between December and February in the Willamette Valley and between November and January in the Rogue Valley--typical pruning times in both regions. Thus, pruning wound protection is necessary to prevent vines from new infections every year. We evaluated pruning wound protection products in two-year field studies. Topsin, Rally, and VitiSeal were found to be the most effective pruning wound protectants in our studies. Furthermore, we identified that the application of systemic fungicides offers longer-term protection during rainy winter conditions in the Pacific Northwest compared to non-treated pruning wounds.

Impact of Pediococcus on Wine Chemical and Sensory Properties

Esther Wanjohi1, Elizabeth Tomasino2, Jaewoo Choi3, Liping Yang4, and James Osborne5
1Graduate Research Assistant, 2Associate Professor, and 5Professor, Department of Food Science and Technology; 3Senior Research Associate and 4Senior Faculty Research Assistant, Department of Chemistry, Oregon State University, Corvallis, OR 97331.
*Corresponding Author: james.osborne@oregonstate.edu, 541-737-6494

Pediococci have been isolated from wines worldwide but their specific impact on wine quality is not well described. This project aimed to characterize the sensory impact of Pediococcus on wine and investigate the effect of wine lees and nutrient additions on biogenic amine (BA) production. Nineteen Pediococcus isolates isolated from Oregon, Washington, and California wines were screened for their impact on the chemical and sensory properties of a 2022 Oregon Pinot noir. Based on the sensory results from this screening, four isolates were selected and inoculated into 2023 Oregon Chardonnay, Merlot, and Pinot noir wines (post-MLF) and grown for nine weeks. Wines were assessed by rate all that applies sensory analysis using an experienced wine panel. A range of descriptors were used for wines inoculated with Pediococcus with many being typical aroma and mouthfeel descriptors while potentially negative terms such as tortilla chip, musty, and cooked vegetable were also used. P. inopinatus OW8 inoculation resulted in significantly different aromas in Chardonnay, Merlot, and Pinot noir wines while P. parvulus WS9 inoculation did not alter wine aroma relative to the control wines.

To explore factors impacting biogenic amine production, different nutrient additions were made to 2021 Chardonnay juice prior to fermentation. After completion of alcoholic fermentation wines were cold settled and dimethyl-dicarbonate was added to eliminate background microorganisms. After forty eight hours wines were racked (no-lees) or not racked (lees treatments), and P. inopinatus OW8 or P. parvulus WS9 were inoculated into the wines. After ten weeks growth samples were collected for BA analysis by LC-MS. The presence of lees supported better growth of the Pediococcus isolates and increased the amount of BA produced. The addition of Nutriferm Arom Plus resulted in elevated levels of putrescine, cadaverine and tyramine while the addition of DAP significantly increased the production of histamine by P. inopinatus OW8. Overall, aging on the lees with the addition of DAP pre-fermentation resulted in the highest levels of histamine in the wine.

Consumer’s Response to Smoke-Impacted Wine: Understanding Liking, Emotions, and Impact of Label Information

Jenna A. Fryer1,4, Amanda Dupas de Matos2,5,6, Joanne Hort3,5,6, Elizabeth Tomasino3,4*

1 Graduate Research Assistant

2 Senior Research Officer, 3 Professor, 4 Department of Food Science and Technology, Oregon State University, 100 Wiegand Hall, Corvallis, OR 97331, USA, 5Food Experience and Sensory Testing (Feast) Laboratory, Orchard Road, Massey University, Palmerston North 4442, New Zealand, 6Riddet Institute, University Avenue, Massey University, Palmerston North 4410, New Zealand

Smoke-impacted wines are described as having smokey, burnt, and dirty aromas and flavors along with a lasting ashy finish. These potent attributes can mask desirable traits, like fruity and floral notes, generally leading to decreased wine quality. While the chemical and sensory effects of smoke on wine have been well researched, few studies have investigated consumer response. The aims of this research were to explore how consumers respond to smoke-impacted wine, confirm whether clusters of consumers exist and characterize them by their liking of smoky flavors in foods/beverages and subjective wine knowledge, and explore how different label concepts influence these responses. Additionally, this work utilized consumers with less exposure to the effects of smoke impact on wine, with New Zealand currently not experiencing the impact of wildfires that has been seen in Australia and the United States. Participants evaluated five blends of a smoke-impacted wine, and the wine presented with four different label concepts. Consumers responded to overall liking, emotions, and perceived attributes. The results identified two clusters of consumers: one disliking the smoke-impacted wine and the other liking the wine. Smoke-likers had a higher liking for smoky flavors in general, along with a higher level of subjective wine knowledge. The smoke-disliker cluster was more susceptible to the impacts of the labels, with the wine being significantly more liked and altering the elicited emotions and attributes. This research provides vital information to the wine industry as they face the challenge of future wildfire years. These findings suggest that the distinct sensory profile of smoke-impacted wines may not universally deter consumers and how labelling can impact this perception. By addressing key knowledge gaps, this study enhances understanding of consumer responses to smoke-impacted wine along with providing valuable guidance to both industry and future research.

Microbial Terroir in Oregon Vineyards – A Case Study of Hanseniaspora 

Chris Curtin1, Tess Snyder2, James Osborne3

1Assoc. Professor, 2MS Graduate, 3Professor, Food Science and Technology, Oregon State University, Address, Corvallis, OR 97331.

*Corresponding Author: christopher.curtin@oregonstate.edu, 541-368-8304

Microbial terroir is a term that was first defined around a decade ago, merging the general concept of wine terroir with observations made using newly available high-throughput sequencing technologies that showed microbial communities in Californian vineyards varied regionally and according to grape varietal. Since that time, microbial terroir studies have been performed around the world, highlighting similarities and differences in vineyard-associated microbial communities, yet our understanding of what constitutes a “good” vineyard microbiome has remained elusive. Differences are interesting, but are they likely to make a difference to the wine?

In this study we made use of high-throughput sequencing to profile the fungal communities associated with organic/biodynamically grown Pinot Noir grapes from twelve vineyards in two sub-AVA’s of the Willamette Valley – the first published study of this kind in Oregon. We detected differences between vineyards and the two sub-AVA’s in terms of overall profiles of fungal species and found that a major driver of those differences was abundance of Hanseniaspora.

Hanseniaspora yeasts are frequently found in vineyards and uninoculated wine fermentations, and depending upon the species present, their contributions to the wine may be positive or negative. For the vineyards sampled in this study, the most common species of Hanseniaspora was the known spoilage organism Hanseniaspora uvarum. Previous work has shown that H. uvarum abundance during the early stages of wine fermentation is increased if a cold-soak is performed, which in turn results in production of undesired volatile aroma compounds. In this study, across more than 100 distinct samples we observed a strong correlation between the H. uvarum abundances in must, cold-soak and early-fermentation.

Taken together, these results show differences in microbial terroir between vineyards and sub-AVAs, and provide an example of how those differences matter.

Analyzing Rootstock Effects on Fruit Set Behavior of Wine Grapes in Southern Oregon

Cristoph Anderson1, Richard Baydo2, Suraj Kar3, and Alexander Levin4*

1 Branch Experiment Station Intern, Department of Forest Ecosystems and Society, Oregon State University, Corvallis, OR, 2 OWRI-EFF Scholar, Department of Horticulture, Oregon State University, Corvallis, OR

3 Assistant Professor, Wine and Viticulture Department, California Polytechnic State University, San Luis Obispo, CA, 4 Associate Professor, Department of Horticulture; Director and Viticulturist, Southern Oregon Research and Extension Center, Oregon State University, Central Point, OR

*Corresponding Author: alexander.levin@oregonstate.edu, (541) 930-8401

Wine grapes (Vitis vinifera L.) are commonly grown on rootstocks to combat soil-borne pests and pathogens, but also to improve adaptation to soil physio-chemical properties and modulate plant vigor. All these qualities may directly or indirectly affect production. However, little research has been done in the modern era to see how these rootstocks can affect fruit set. Thus, this study was initiated to investigate the interaction effect of rootstock on fruit set of two leading red wine grape cultivars under field conditions. The study was initiated in 2024 at OSU’s Southern Oregon Research and Extension Center in a 2.2-acre vineyard, where 10 rootstocks of both scion cultivars were planted in 2019. The two scion cultivars – Pinot noir and Cabernet Sauvignon – grafted to 10 different rootstocks were planted in randomized complete block design (RCBD) with a split-plot factorial treatment structure with five replications. Scion cultivars were arranged in main plots, and rootstocks were in subplots. Experimental units consisted of three vines. Fruit set was determined by covering two inflorescences per vine with mesh bags prior to flowering and collected one week after fruit set. The caps were weighed, and the berries were counted in the lab. Results showed that there was a significant interaction effect between rootstock and scion (p < 0.0001). Fruit set in Pinot noir showed more significant variation, ranging from 46.6% to 29.6%, while Cabernet Sauvignon ranged from 38.3% to 28.1%. The rootstock with the highest fruit set percent in both Pinot noir and Cabernet Sauvignon was 5C, with the lowest fruit set percentage in both being St. George. In addition, there was a statistically significant linear decrease in the percent fruit set with increasing flower number per inflorescence. The study will be repeated in subsequent years to confirm the accuracy of our results.

Phenological Development In Grapevines: Limited Interaction Between Scion Cultivar And Rootstock

Richard Baydo1, Cristoph Anderson2, Suraj Kar3, and Alexander Levin4*

1 OWRI-EFF Scholar, Department of Horticulture, Oregon State University, Corvallis, OR

2 Branch Experiment Station Intern, Department of Forest Ecosystems and Society, Oregon State University, Corvallis, OR, 3 Assistant Professor, Wine and Viticulture Department, California Polytechnic State University, San Luis Obispo, CA4 Associate Professor, Department of Horticulture; Director and Viticulturist, Southern Oregon Research and Extension Center, Oregon State University, Central Point, OR

*Corresponding Author: alexander.levin@oregonstate.edu, (541) 930-8401

Phenological development in grapevines is primarily determined by scion cultivar but can be influenced by rootstock. However, the degree of rootstock impact varies with vineyard environment, and there is limited quantitative data on scion–rootstock interactions under field conditions in Southern Oregon. This study used a rootstock trial at the Southern Oregon Research and Extension Center (SOREC) to evaluate how scion and rootstock interact to influence phenological development. The trial was conducted in a 2.2-acre vineyard planted in 2019 with two scion cultivars – Pinot noir and Cabernet Sauvignon – grafted onto 10 rootstocks, each replicated five times. Field plots followed a randomized complete block design with a split-plot factorial structure: scions were randomized in main plots, rootstocks in subplots. Each experimental unit consisted of three vines. Prior to budbreak, two spurs per vine were flagged and monitored weekly from March through August. Phenological stages were recorded using the modified Eichorn-Lorenz system. There were no statistically significant interactions between scion and rootstock at any phenological stage. Cabernet Sauvignon developed significantly later than Pinot noir across all stages (p < 0.001), with budbreak, bloom, and veraison occurring four, five, and eight days later, respectively. Rootstock had a smaller but significant effect on bloom and veraison only (p < 0.05), with differences among rootstocks ranging from 1–3 days. First-year results indicate that scion cultivar is the primary driver of phenological development, with minimal influence from rootstock and no interaction between the two. The study will continue in subsequent years to validate and expand on these findings.

Pinot Noir Potassium Status Varies With Different Rootstocks

Grace E. Lilly1 and Patricia A. Skinkis2

1Graduate Research Assistant and 2Professor & Viticulture Extension Specialist, Department of Horticulture, Oregon State University, 2750 SW Campus Way, Corvallis, OR 97331.

*Corresponding Author: , 541-737-1411

Grapevine rootstocks are known to have different potassium (K) affinities. Most Oregon vineyards are planted to vines grafted on three primary rootstocks: 3309C, Riparia Gloire, and 101-14. Rootstocks with V. rupestris parentage (101-14 and 3309C) are known to have high K affinity. High K concentration in must can reduce titratable acidity and raise pH, and pH>3.65 can jeopardize microbial stability during fermentation. To understand the relationship between rootstocks and potassium status, Pinot noir (Wädenswil, FPS 2A) on eight rootstocks and own-rooted vines were evaluated during 2024. Rootstocks included 3309C, 1103P, 44-53, 5BB, Schwarzmann, 101-14, Riparia Gloire, 420A, and own-rooted vines. Leaf blade and petiole K were measured at verasion, and juice K concentration was measured from ripening to harvest. Data were also collected on vine growth, yield, pruning weights, and fruit composition. Results show 1103P had higher juice K concentrations than Riparia Gloire, 3309C, and 101-14 at harvest. Pinot noir on 44-53 and 3309C had higher verasion leaf blade K concentrations compared to Pinot noir on 1103P, Riparia Gloire, 420A, and 5BB. In fact, Pinot noir on 5BB reached near deficient leaf blade K concentrations at verasion. When comparing all rootstock combinations, higher yield was correlated with higher juice K. Pruning weight was negatively correlated with verasion leaf blade K concentration, and this is largely explained by 5BB with larger vine sizes and lower K affinity. To monitor K in Pinot noir juice during ripening, a subset of four rootstocks were selected (101-14, 3309C, 420A, and Riparia Gloire), representing different K affinities, but there were no differences. This work will continue for two more seasons. Information from this research is building upon regional rootstock performance metrics that will assist growers in selecting rootstocks for future plantings.

Funding source: Oregon Wine Board

Evaluating Rootstocks For Cool-Climate White Wine Grape Production

Abigail G. Sriram1, Grace E. Lilly2, and Patricia A. Skinkis3*

1Faculty Research Assistant, 2Graduate Student, and 3Professor and Viticulture Extension Specialist, Department of Horticulture, Oregon State University, 2750 SW Campus Way, Corvallis, OR 97331

*Corresponding Author: , 541-737-1411

Oregon vineyards have been planted to few rootstocks that have limited adaptation to dry-farming. Alternative rootstocks for the region are of interest as growers consider future plantings. To understand rootstock impact on vine growth and productivity under dry-farmed conditions, a three-year research trial (2023-2025) is being conducted at OSU Woodhall Vineyard. The trial was planted in 1997 to Chardonnay (Dijon 95) and Pinot gris (Colmar 146) grafted to nine rootstocks (101-14, 3309, Riparia Gloire (RG), 110R, 420A, 5BB, Gravesac, 44-53 and SO4) and own-rooted vines. Results from 2023-2024 show no differences in bud break, bloom, or veraison. However, rootstock led to differences in cultivar yield, vegetative vigor, tissue macronutrients and fruit composition. The highest vine size and yields were on 5BB, SO4, and 420A while vines on RG, 3309, and 44-53 had the smallest vine size and yields. Vines on these latter rootstocks generally had statistically higher total soluble solids (TSS), higher pH, and lower titratable acidity (TA) at harvest. Vines on RG and 44-53 had higher TSS (23.8-23.9°Brix) than vines on 420A, SO4, 5BB, 110R, and 101-14 (21.0-22.6°Brix). Fewer differences were observed for pH and TA, though vines on RG had higher pH (3.43 vs. 3.31) and lower TA (5.5 vs. 7.2 g/L) than 420A. Cultivars differed in rootstock response to water stress, but vines on RG, 3309, and 110R had the highest water stress while vines on 5BB and SO4 experienced the least water stress. Stem water potential was positively correlated with pruning weight and leaf area index for both cultivars, showing less water stress in vines with larger canopies. There were differences in veraison leaf blade potassium but not nitrogen. These macronutrient differences may be explained by rootstock impacts on vine size and fruit yield and will be explored further.

Influence of Delayed Irrigation Initiation Time on Pinot noir Wine Composition and Volatile Compounds


Tingting Ye1, Alexander Levin2, Yanping L Qian3, Michael Qian4*

1Graduate Research Assistant, Department of Food Science and Technology, Oregon State University, 100 Wiegand Hall,3051 SW Campus Way, Corvallis, OR 97331

2Associate Professor, Department of Horticulture, Oregon State University, 4017 Agriculture and Life Science Building, 2750 SW Campus Way, Corvallis, OR 97331

3Associate Professor (Sr. Res), Department of Crop and Soil Science, Oregon State University, 109 Crop Science Building, 3050 SW Campus Way, Corvallis, OR 97331

4Professor, Department of Food Science and Technology, Oregon State University, 100 Wiegand Hall, 3051 SW Campus Way, Corvallis, OR 97331

* Corresponding Author: michael.qian@oregonstate.edu, 541-737-9114

The influence of delayed irrigation initiation time on Pinot noir wine composition was investigated from 2021 to 2023 in three sub-regions with different soil types and climates in Southern Oregon. Grapevines were irrigated with 70% evapotranspiration (ETc) at different initiation times based on normalized vine water status thresholds (ΔSWP) throughout berry development. The ΔSWP was defined as the departure of measure midday stem water potential from the calculated non-water-stressed baseline (ΔSWP = SWPns – SWPabs). Total phenolic contents, phenolic composition, and volatile compounds in wine were analyzed. The results showed a reduction in total phenolic contents in wines with delayed irrigation initiation time in three regions in all three vintages, and an overall downward trend in total monomeric anthocyanin contents in Ashland and Eagle Point but an opposite trend in Jacksonville. Delayed irrigation initiation time greatly impacted volatile compounds in wines, although geographic locations and vintages were more impactful. Methoxypyrazines, associated with herbal, green aroma attribute, decreased with delayed irrigation in Jacksonville and Eagle Point in all three vintages, but varied in Ashland.  Total esters and total terpene alcohols increased in response to delayed irrigation initiation time in all three sites. β-damascenone and β-ionone, associated with berry aroma attribute, increased in response to delayed irrigation initiation time in Jacksonville and Ashland across three years, but not in Eagle Point. Increase in fruity esters, β-damascenone and β-ionone, and decrease in methoxypyrazines, could suggest wines with fruitier, berry-like aroma attributes.

The State Of Vineyard Nutrition In The Us: Survey Findings And Future Directions

Karmandeep K. Sidhu¹ and Patricia A. Skinkis²

¹Graduate Research Assistant, ²Professor & Viticulture Extension Specialist, Department of Horticulture, Oregon State University, 4017 Agricultural & Life Sciences Bldg, Corvallis, OR 97331.

*Corresponding Author: , 541-737-1411

Optimizing vineyard nutrient management is critical for maintaining grapevine health, fruit quality, and long-term sustainability. The High-Resolution (HiRes) Vineyard Nutrient Management Project aimed to develop new nutrient monitoring methods for precision nutrient management. Botryosphaeria dieback is a Grapevine Trunk Disease (GTD) causing cankers and dieback globally and in Oregon vineyards. Delayed pruning and pruning wound protectants are effective strategies to manage GTDs. However, late pruning is often not possible in Oregon due to labor, and rain is ever present. A study was conducted to assess the impact of pruning timing and necessity of repeated fungicide applications. The project was conducted in the Willamette Valley and Rogue Valley over two years. Vines were pruned in either December, January or February and treated with a pruning would protectant (Topsin or VitiSeal) the day of pruning and either just that day or had repeat applications monthly through February. Dothiorella iberica at 2.5 x 104 spores/ml was spray-inoculated on treated wounds during each pruning date. Spur tissues were sampled in August and cultured in half strength PDA amended with antibiotics for pathogen isolation. Percent recovery of D. iberica was calculated and subjected to analysis of variance. In all pruning months, one application of Topsin reduced pathogen recovery as much as repeated applications with average recovery ranging from 0 to 18.8%. Three times (12%) and two times (19.3%) application of VitiSeal reduced pathogen recovery compared to one time application (29.7%) and non-treated control (57.3%) in December. Pathogen recovery was similar in two- and one- time application of VitiSeal in January but was lower than the non-treated control. For February samples, the single VitiSeal application reduced pathogen recovery compared to the non-treated control. Fruit yield and pruning weight were not affected by the treatments applied in any of the pruning timings. Results suggest that a single Topsin application post-pruning is sufficient to reduce D. iberica infections while repeated applications of VitiSeal are required to reduce pathogen recovery compared to single application.
